## LESSON 1: Guard Basics & Posture
*Techniques: #58 Posture, fundamentals of closed guard position*

---

### Q31
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #58 Posture | **Difficulty:** 1

You just got pulled into your opponent's closed guard. What is the FIRST thing you establish?

A) Grab their collar with both hands  
B) Try to open their legs immediately  
C) Sit up straight, toes on the ground, hips driven forward ✓  
D) Post both hands on their chest and push  

**Why C is correct:** Pedro Sauer #58 — posture is the prerequisite for everything. Back straight, toes on ground, hips forward. Without posture you are immediately vulnerable to every guard attack.  
**Why A is wrong:** Grabbing collar without posture means you're leaning forward into their attacks.  
**Why B is wrong:** You cannot safely open the guard from a broken posture.  
**Why D is wrong:** Posting both hands removes your hip base and posture simultaneously.

---

### Q32
**Type:** True-False | **Source:** #58 Posture | **Difficulty:** 1

**True or False:** In your opponent's closed guard, keeping your chin tucked to your chest protects you from chokes.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** Pedro Sauer #58 — "Head up (do not tuck chin)." Tucking the chin actually gives the opponent a frame to work their choke against. Head up and back straight is the correct posture position. A tucked chin does not protect against collar chokes — it only limits your vision and posture.

---

### Q33
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #58 Posture | **Difficulty:** 2

You are in your opponent's closed guard with good posture. They are trying to break your posture by pulling on your collar. What body part is your primary tool to resist?

A) Your arms — extend them to create distance  
B) Your hips — drive them forward to counteract the pulling force ✓  
C) Your legs — drive your knees into the mat for stability  
D) Your neck — flex it to resist the collar pull  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #58 — "Driving hips to ground/forward when opponent attempts to pull you forward with legs." The hips are the engine of posture. Arms alone cannot resist the combined pull of legs and collar grips.  
**Why A is wrong:** Extending arms forward creates a stiff-arm that opponent can use as a lever to pull you down.  
**Why C is wrong:** Driving knees into the mat helps stability but doesn't address the pulling force on your upper body.  
**Why D is wrong:** Neck flexion alone will not resist the pull — and creates a strained position.

---

### Q34
**Type:** Concept | **Source:** #58 | **Difficulty:** 2

Why do toes on the ground matter when you're in your opponent's closed guard?

A) It prevents them from sweeping your legs  
B) It allows you to stand up faster to pass the guard  
C) It activates your hip extension and keeps your base stable ✓  
D) It positions your feet to hook their legs if needed  

**Why C is correct:** Pedro Sauer #58 — "toes on ground" is listed as part of proper posture. Toes on ground allows you to push through your legs and drive hips forward. Flat feet or knees off the ground remove the base of the posture structure.  
**Why A is wrong:** Toes position doesn't directly prevent sweeps.  
**Why B is wrong:** Standing up to pass requires a specific sequence — posture toes are not specifically for this.  
**Why D is wrong:** Your toes are not used to hook when you're kneeling in closed guard.

---

### Q35
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #58, #59 | **Difficulty:** 2

You are in closed guard with good posture. Your opponent grabs your collar with one hand. Before you apply choke protection, what must remain intact?

A) You must keep both hands free  
B) Your posture must remain established ✓  
C) You must have your hips elevated  
D) You must keep your legs outside their guard  

**Why B is correct:** Choke protection (#59) is built on posture (#58). The arm-over-arm defense requires you to have a stable base to block from. If posture is lost first, the choke protection becomes reactive and weak.  
**Why A is wrong:** Choke protection requires using one arm to block — you don't need both free.  
**Why C is wrong:** Elevated hips is a guard attack position, not relevant here.  
**Why D is wrong:** Your legs are inside their closed guard by definition.

### Q36
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #59 | **Difficulty:** 2

Your opponent has their first hand deep in your collar from inside their guard. You bring your arm over their choking arm. What do you do with your elbow specifically?

A) Keep your elbow high and pointed upward  
B) Drop or flare your elbow to block the incoming second grip ✓  
C) Drive your elbow into their chest  
D) Keep your elbow close to your own body  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #59 — "drop or flare elbow to block 2nd grip, control and prevent hip movement." The elbow position is the active blocking mechanism for the second hand. Without the elbow drop, the second grip can sneak in.  
**Why A is wrong:** High elbow creates a gap for the second grip to enter.  
**Why C is wrong:** Driving elbow into chest is a different technique — not choke protection.  
**Why D is wrong:** Elbow close to body doesn't cover the space the second grip is entering from.

### Q37
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #60 Comb the Hair | **Difficulty:** 1

When executing "comb the hair" to defend a collar choke — which hand slides under the choking hand?

A) Your opposite hand (cross body)  
B) Your same-side hand as the choke ✓  
C) Both hands simultaneously  
D) It doesn't matter — either hand works  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #60 — "Use your hand on the side of the initiated choke." The same-side hand slides under the choking hand because it's the shortest path and maintains posture better than reaching across with the opposite hand.  
**Why A is wrong:** Opposite hand requires crossing your body which compromises your posture.  
**Why C is wrong:** Using both hands removes all your posture support.  
**Why D is wrong:** Hand choice is specific — same side is the correct answer.

---

### Q38
**Type:** True-False | **Source:** #60 | **Difficulty:** 2

**True or False:** "Squeeze the bread" and "comb the hair" are two defenses for the same situation — both work equally well against any collar grip.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** They are complements, not equivalents. "Comb the hair" is best when opponent has ONE hand in the collar — you slide your hand under and circle your head out. "Squeeze the bread" is described as effective when opponent has TWO hands in the collar threatening the choke — you use a fist in the solar plexus as counter-pressure. Knowing which to apply when is part of the skill.

### Q40
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #60, #61 | **Difficulty:** 3

You are in your opponent's closed guard. They have a guillotine choke set up — arm around your neck — and they begin to lay back to apply it. What is the correct defense?

A) Try to posture up hard and rip your head out  
B) Wrap your arm around their back and fall to the side they have the choke ✓  
C) Open your guard and step back  
D) Try to reach your arm over their arm to break the grip  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #61 — "Guillotine choke defense: arm over shoulder and fall to side, wrap arm around the back, go forward and climb legs high, slide legs off hips, hips to ground and extract legs." Wrapping and falling to the side changes the angle of the guillotine and creates the escape pathway.  
**Why A is wrong:** Posturing up into a guillotine tightens the choke — more pressure on the neck.  
**Why C is wrong:** Opening your guard while being guillotined removes hip control needed for the escape.  
**Why D is wrong:** Reaching over their arm to break the grip is difficult against a locked guillotine grip.

---

### Q41
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #61 Guillotine Defense | **Difficulty:** 3

You are caught in a guillotine from inside your opponent's guard. You've wrapped your arm around their back. What do you do with your legs next?

A) Keep your legs outside their guard and push off the floor  
B) Climb your legs high on their body to remove the choke leverage ✓  
C) Open your guard and try to escape sideways  
D) Drive your free arm into their hip to create distance  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #61 — "go forward and climb legs high, slide legs off hips, hips to ground and extract legs." Climbing the legs high redistributes weight onto their upper body and takes the squeeze out of the choke. Then you slide legs off and escape.  
**Why A is wrong:** Keeping legs outside removes the body control needed for the escape sequence.  
**Why C is wrong:** Opening guard at this stage removes the climbing platform.  
**Why D is wrong:** Pushing hip with free arm creates distance that works against the necessary wrapping motion.

## LESSON 3: Scissor Sweep Mastery
*Technique: #70 — full depth*

---

### Q42
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #70 | **Difficulty:** 1

What is the grip setup for the scissor sweep?

A) Double collar grips — both hands in the collar  
B) Pistol grip on sleeve and collar grip ✓  
C) Grip on both sleeves  
D) Under-hook on one arm and collar grip  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #70 — "pistol grip sleeve, pull them to you to stretch them out." The sleeve grip creates the arm control while the collar grip controls posture direction. This is the standard and correct setup.  
**Why A is wrong:** Double collar leaves no sleeve control — opponent's arm is free to post and block.  
**Why C is wrong:** Double sleeve limits your ability to break posture.  
**Why D is wrong:** Under-hook is a half guard concept — not the scissor sweep grip.

---

### Q43
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #70 | **Difficulty:** 2

In the scissor sweep, what does the leg positioned at the opponent's hip do?

A) Creates a hook that blocks the hip from moving sideways  
B) Provides a platform to push off during the sweep ✓  
C) Wraps around their leg to block their base  
D) Stays passive — only the knee-to-shoulder leg does the work  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #70 — "drop foot off opponent's hip to ground and scissor your legs to turn opponent's back to ground." The hip leg creates the scissoring action — the mechanical force that turns them. Without it, you only have one side of the scissors.  
**Why A is wrong:** Blocking the hip from moving is not the function — the hip leg drives the rotation.  
**Why C is wrong:** Wrapping the leg is a different technique (hook sweep).  
**Why D is wrong:** Both legs work — the knee-to-shoulder creates the angle, the hip leg creates the sweep force.

---

### Q44
**Type:** True-False | **Source:** #70 | **Difficulty:** 2

**True or False:** You can successfully execute the scissor sweep without first shrimping to the side.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** Pedro Sauer #70 — "shrimp to side opposite of your control grips, connect knee pointed at opponent's shoulder with feet clamping on both sides." Without shrimping, you cannot position your knee at their shoulder — it will stay too low. The shrimp creates the angle that makes the scissor mechanic possible.

---

### Q45
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #70 | **Difficulty:** 3

You attempt a scissor sweep but your opponent posts their free hand on the mat to prevent the sweep. What is the correct response?

A) Give up the sweep and return to closed guard  
B) Try harder with more force  
C) Use the posted hand as a kimura setup ✓  
D) Switch to a triangle — one arm is now extended  

**Why C is correct:** When the opponent posts their hand on the mat, this is the classic kimura trigger (#66 — "opponent puts their hand on the floor"). The sweep attempt created the opportunity. This is BJJ flow — one attack creates the next.  
**Why A is wrong:** Giving up position unnecessarily — a better option exists.  
**Why B is wrong:** More force rarely works against a solid post — use the new information instead.  
**Why D is wrong:** Triangle requires one arm in and one arm out — a posted arm doesn't create this.

---

### Q46
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #70 | **Difficulty:** 2

After completing the scissor sweep and landing in mount, your opponent immediately bridges to escape. What is your priority?

A) Hold mount with your arms  
B) Swim your arms through to maintain mount ✓  
C) Go straight for a cross choke before they can escape  
D) Move to side control — it's easier to hold  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #49/23 — "Maintain mount: swimming through arms" when opponent tries to push you off. This directly addresses the bridge. Maintaining mount position opens all attack options.  
**Why A is wrong:** Arms alone cannot hold mount — they need to swim and adjust.  
**Why C is wrong:** Going for a choke during a bridge means your base is compromised during the submission attempt.  
**Why D is wrong:** Voluntarily leaving mount is unnecessary — you've earned a dominant position.

## LESSON 4: Hook Sweep & Sit-Up Sweep
*Techniques: #71, #72*

### Q47
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #71 | **Difficulty:** 2

Your opponent is in your guard and steps one knee up. To initiate the hook sweep, where do you focus your weight?

A) Equally on both sides  
B) On their posted leg (the one still kneeling) ✓  
C) On the leg that stepped up  
D) Forward toward their chest  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #71 — "hang weight on support leg." The support leg (the one still on the ground) is where you focus weight to make it heavy before hooking the stepped-up knee. This unbalances them before the actual sweep.  
**Why A is wrong:** Equal weight distribution doesn't load the support leg enough.  
**Why C is wrong:** Hanging on the stepped-up leg has no mechanical advantage for this sweep.  
**Why D is wrong:** Leaning forward is for sit-up sweep — not hook sweep.

---

### Q48
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #71 | **Difficulty:** 2

In the hook sweep, after focusing weight on the support leg, what do you do to create the hook?

A) Grab their knee with your hand  
B) Place your foot behind their stepped-up knee  
C) Rotate your leg at the knee to create a hook under their thigh ✓  
D) Hook your foot around their ankle  

**Why C is correct:** Pedro Sauer #71 — "hook leg by rotating your leg at the knee, bring opponent forward, sweep and mount." The hook is created by rotating the leg — it's a leg movement, not a hand grab.  
**Why A is wrong:** Using your hand removes your grip control and is not the described mechanic.  
**Why B is wrong:** Foot behind the stepped-up knee is the wrong position — you hook under their thigh.  
**Why D is wrong:** Ankle hook is a different technique — too low for this sweep.

---

### Q49
**Type:** True-False | **Source:** #71 | **Difficulty:** 2

**True or False:** The hook sweep and the scissor sweep both work best against an opponent with the same body posture.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** They target different situations. Hook sweep — opponent steps one knee up (asymmetric base). Scissor sweep — opponent has both knees on the ground, neutral posture. Applying hook sweep against a flat-kneeling opponent gives you nothing to hook. Applying scissor sweep when they've stepped up loses the scissor angle.

**Why B is wrong:** Hand on mat is the kimura trigger (#66).  
**Why D is wrong:** Standing up to pass creates a different situation — standing guard pass defense.

### Q53
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #65 Triangle | **Difficulty:** 1

For a triangle choke to work, the arm position must be:

A) Both opponent's arms inside your legs  
B) Both opponent's arms outside your legs  
C) One arm inside your legs, one arm outside ✓  
D) The arm position doesn't matter — just lock your legs  

**Why C is correct:** Pedro Sauer #65 — "one arm in, one arm out." The triangle requires one arm and the neck to be inside the triangle. With both arms in or both out, the choke has no pressure on the carotid arteries.  
**Why A is wrong:** Both arms inside gives opponent a defensive frame — the choke is ineffective.  
**Why B is wrong:** Both arms outside means there's no arm in the triangle — no pressure.  
**Why D is wrong:** Arm position is the fundamental requirement of the triangle — it always matters.

### Q54
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #65 Triangle | **Difficulty:** 2

You have your legs in a triangle position but the choke isn't finishing. Your opponent is defending by stacking you. What do you do?

A) Straighten your legs to break the stack  
B) Pull their head down toward your chest ✓  
C) Try to open the triangle and go for an armbar  
D) Uncross your ankles to release pressure and reset  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #65 — "toes up and pull head." Pulling the head down counters the stacking defense by bringing the opponent's posture toward you. This re-applies the triangle pressure even as they stack.  
**Why A is wrong:** Straightening legs releases the triangle lock.  
**Why C is wrong:** Abandoning a live triangle for a reset loses position unnecessarily.  
**Why D is wrong:** Uncrossing ankles removes the triangle entirely.

### Q56
**Type:** True-False | **Source:** #64 Armbar from Guard | **Difficulty:** 2

**True or False:** To finish an armbar from guard, you should keep your toes pointed downward to generate more hip extension force.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** Pedro Sauer #64 — "raise hips and calf on back of neck — keep toes pointed UP." Toes pointed up tightens the leg triangle around the opponent's arm, squeezing it in place. Toes down loosens the leg position and reduces control of the arm.

### Q57

You are attempting an armbar from guard. You have control of one side, your hips are raised and calf is on the back of their neck. What brings the leg over their head?

**Why A is wrong:** Kicking over forcefully risks losing the arm control and hip position.  
**Why C is wrong:** Using hands to position the leg removes grip control.

### Q58
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #64, #65, #66 | **Difficulty:** 3

You have control of your opponent's right arm from guard. They try to escape by pulling their arm out toward the right. What is the best response?

A) Hold tighter with both hands

**Why A is wrong:** Holding tighter with hands alone doesn't address the directional escape.  
**Why C is wrong:** Releasing a live armbar setup unnecessarily loses the dominant position.

### Q59
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #66 | **Difficulty:** 1

The kimura from guard is triggered when your opponent does what specific thing?

A) Grabs your collar  
B) Tries to stand up to pass  
C) Places their hand on the mat ✓  
D) Postures up with both hands on your hips  

**Why C is correct:** Pedro Sauer #66 — "opponent is in your guard and you notice they put their hand on the floor." This is the specific trigger. The hand on the floor is an isolated arm — the kimura target.  
**Why A is wrong:** Collar grab triggers choke protection, not kimura.  
**Why B is wrong:** Standing up triggers standing guard pass defense.  
**Why D is wrong:** Both hands on hips is a guard pass posture — different situation.

---

### Q60
**Type:** True-False | **Source:** #66 | **Difficulty:** 2

**True or False:** In the kimura from guard, you cross your feet over your opponent's back before establishing the figure four grip.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** Pedro Sauer #66 — sequence is: sit up → drive body up → trap foot → move hips toward hand → FIGURE FOUR GRIPS → lay down → cross feet over back → apply lock. The figure four grip comes before crossing the feet. The feet cross after you lay back down.

### Q61
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #66 | **Difficulty:** 2

You have established a figure four (kimura) grip on your opponent's arm from closed guard. Why do you need to scoot your hips away before applying the lock?

A) To create distance so they can't grab your collar  
B) To isolate their arm and remove the possibility of them using their body to resist ✓  
C) To give yourself room to cross your feet  
D) To position yourself for a triangle if the kimura fails  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #66 — "scoot away to isolate arms, cross feet over back." Scooting away takes their arm away from their body's support structure — an arm connected to their hip can resist. An isolated arm cannot.  
**Why A is wrong:** Collar distance is not the purpose of scooting.  
**Why C is wrong:** Foot crossing happens after scooting, but that's not why you scoot.  
**Why D is wrong:** Triangle position would require different body alignment.

---

### Q62
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #67 Guillotine | **Difficulty:** 2

When circling your arm around the opponent's neck for the guillotine from guard — how should your hand be oriented?

A) Palm facing toward the ceiling  
B) Fingers pointing down, hand straight ✓  
C) Fist clenched tightly  
D) Palm facing toward their shoulder  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #67 — "keep hand straight as you wrap." A straight hand slides cleanly under/around the neck. A curved or clenched hand creates friction and doesn't seat properly.  
**Why A is wrong:** Palm up during the wrapping creates an awkward angle.  
**Why C is wrong:** Fist during the wrap makes it harder to get depth.  
**Why D is wrong:** Palm toward shoulder misdirects the wrapping motion.

### Q63
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #67 | **Difficulty:** 3

You have a guillotine from guard. You close your guard and begin applying the choke. Your opponent is defending by gripping your arm to relieve pressure. What do you do?

A) Open your guard to create more pulling force  
B) Keep your legs on top of their legs and lay back ✓  
C) Try to switch to a cross choke  
D) Release the guillotine and go for a sweep  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #67 — "close guard and apply choke, lay back to ground, bring legs up to wrap full guard." The closing guard and laying back creates the combination of neck pressure and hip control. Their arm defense cannot overcome combined neck and hip pressure.  
**Why A is wrong:** Opening guard removes the hip pressure that is half the choke mechanism.  
**Why C is wrong:** Abandoning a live guillotine to reset loses momentum.  
**Why D is wrong:** Releasing to sweep from a live guillotine is backwards — finish the submission.

### Q64
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #68 Kneeling Pass | **Difficulty:** 2

In the kneeling shoulder pass, after your back leg steps straight and knee is up, what do you do with your arms?

A) Post both hands on their hips  
B) Put one arm inside their leg and grab the lapel ✓  
C) Grab both their ankles  
D) Reach under their back  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #68 — "put arm inside leg, hold lapel pull elbow to your hip, bring opposite hand under leg and place on floor." The inside arm controls their leg while the lapel grip controls posture. Both are needed for the shoulder pass.  
**Why A is wrong:** Posting on hips doesn't set up the shoulder pass mechanics.  
**Why C is wrong:** Grabbing ankles is a different pass style entirely.  
**Why D is wrong:** Reaching under back has no mechanical purpose in this pass.

### Q65
**Type:** True-False | **Source:** #68 | **Difficulty:** 2

**True or False:** During the kneeling guard pass, you should keep your elbows wide to create maximum width for the pass.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** Pedro Sauer #68 — "keep elbows inside." Elbows inside prevent the opponent from creating frames against your shoulders and body. Elbows wide create gaps for the opponent to frame and recover guard.

### Q66
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #68 | **Difficulty:** 3

You're doing the kneeling shoulder pass and you have their leg on your shoulder. Their guard is now open. They try to recover their guard by bringing their leg back. What is the correct action?

A) Let them recover — reestablish posture and try again

C) Step back and attempt the standing guard pass instead

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #68 — "secure leg apply weight forward, grab collar and drop your weight to pass, turn your body around hip with consistent pressure." Weight commitment combined with collar grip prevents guard recovery.  
**Why A is wrong:** Allowing guard recovery resets the entire sequence.  
**Why C is wrong:** Switching to standing pass while you have positional advantage is unnecessary.  
**Why D is wrong:** Pushing leg down removes it from your shoulder — you need it there for the pass.

### Q67
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #69 Standing Pass | **Difficulty:** 2

In the standing guard pass, after grabbing both lapels and standing — what is the key action that opens the guard?

A) Step your knees outside their hips  
B) Drag the opponent under you by lifting them ✓  
C) Push their knees apart with your hands  
D) Turn your body sideways to break their leg lock  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #69 — "stand and drag opponent under, pinch and control legs, use elbow to open guard." Dragging them under changes the angle of their closed guard and sets up the elbow opening.  
**Why A is wrong:** Stepping outside while still in closed guard is not mechanically described here.  
**Why C is wrong:** Pushing knees apart with hands is not the described mechanic for this pass.  
**Why D is wrong:** Turning sideways is not the described approach.

What is the most common mistake white belts make when stuck in someone's closed guard?

B) Trying to open the guard before establishing posture ✓  
C) Being too passive and not attempting passes

**Why B is correct:** This is the universal consensus from Pedro Sauer curriculum and expert sources. Posture (#58) is the first technique listed in guard defense for a reason. Without posture, any guard opening attempt puts you at maximum risk of every guard attack simultaneously.  
**Why A is wrong:** Attempting submissions is a top player problem — from inside closed guard as bottom it's sweeps and submissions.  
**Why C is wrong:** Passivity is a problem but it's secondary to the posture error.  
**Why D is wrong:** Grip strength isn't the primary error — technique and sequence are.

### Q70
**Type:** Concept | **Source:** #70, #71, #72 | **Difficulty:** 2

What does "reading the opponent's energy" mean in the context of closed guard sweeps?

A) Watching their face to predict their next move  
B) Identifying which direction their weight is distributed and selecting the sweep that exploits it ✓  
C) Feeling their grip strength to know when to sweep  
D) Waiting for them to make a technique mistake  

**Why B is correct:** Each sweep has an energy trigger. Rearward energy → sit-up sweep. Stepped knee → hook sweep. Neutral/forward → scissor sweep. Recognizing the energy is the skill that makes sweeps work — you pick the sweep that matches what they're giving you.  
**Why A is wrong:** Face reading isn't the mechanism — body position is.  
**Why C is wrong:** Grip strength isn't the sweep trigger.  
**Why D is wrong:** Waiting for mistakes is passive — you should create the energy you want to exploit.

### Q71
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #63, #64, #65, #66 | **Difficulty:** 3

You are in closed guard. You attempt a triangle but your opponent postures up and defends. Their right arm is now extended and their left arm is inside. What is the best immediate transition?

A) Go back to scissor sweep  
B) Switch to cross choke — they've opened their collar  
C) Transition to armbar on their extended right arm ✓  
D) Try the triangle again with more leg squeeze  

**Why C is correct:** The armbar from guard is the natural complement to a failed triangle when one arm is extended. Pedro Sauer #64 — "control one side of body, lift hips, climb and clamp." The triangle setup already has you in position to transition — the extended arm is the target.  
**Why A is wrong:** Scissor sweep requires resetting completely — you lose the advantageous position.  
**Why B is wrong:** Cross choke requires different body positioning and collar grips from a fresh setup.  
**Why D is wrong:** More squeeze on a properly defended triangle doesn't change the outcome.

### Q72
**Type:** True-False | **Source:** #63, #65, #66 | **Difficulty:** 2

**True or False:** Breaking your opponent's posture is always required before attempting a submission from closed guard.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** Some submissions work better with posture broken (cross choke — easier collar access), but others work against good posture or in specific circumstances. The kimura is triggered by the opponent's hand on the floor (often happens WITH good posture during a pass attempt). The triangle can be set up as they posture up if the arm positioning is right. Posture breaking is essential for collar attacks — not universally required for all submissions.

### Q73

B) Scissor sweep — create the scissor position ✓  
C) Triangle — their arms are in and out  
D) Kimura — their hands are extended forward  

**Why B is correct:** Hands on hips is a guard-passing posture. The opponent's energy is forward/neutral. This is the classic scissor sweep setup — you shrimp to the side and create the scissor angle against their neutral weight. Collar access is limited (hands on hips, not in collar). Triangle is very hard against this posture. Kimura from guard requires a hand on the floor.  
**Why A is wrong:** Collar grips are not available — opponent's hands are on your hips.  
**Why C is wrong:** Triangle against straight-up posture with both arms in front is very low percentage.  
**Why D is wrong:** Hands on hips are not the same as hand on the floor — the kimura trigger is a posted floor hand.

### Q74

Why do BJJ instructors say "guard is not a defensive position — it's an offensive one"?

C) Because it's easier to submit from guard than from top  
D) Because the person in guard controls when the fight ends  

**Why B is correct:** From closed guard you control the distance, break posture, attack with sweeps and 5 submissions, and dictate pace. **Why A is wrong:** Guard doesn't guarantee winning — it creates offensive opportunity.  
**Why C is wrong:** Statistics don't support this — it's about the principle not the statistic.

### Q75
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #65, #64, #66 | **Difficulty:** 3

You are in closed guard. You attempt a triangle but your opponent defends by grabbing their own collar with their free arm to resist. This creates what?

A) A sweep opportunity — they've used their arm for defense  
B) A kimura opportunity on the defending arm ✓  
C) A cross choke — their collar grip opens their lapel  
D) Nothing — reset and try again  

**Why B is correct:** When opponent grabs their own collar to defend the triangle, that arm is now isolated and in a bent position — classic kimura territory. This is BJJ chain thinking — one submission's defense creates the next submission's setup.  
**Why A is wrong:** A sweep requires different positioning than a triangle attempt in progress.  
**Why C is wrong:** The collar being touched by their own hand doesn't create cross choke access.  
**Why D is wrong:** Never reset when a submission chain exists.

### Q76
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #70, #66 | **Difficulty:** 3

You are in closed guard. You attempt a scissor sweep. Your opponent posts their left hand on the mat to prevent it. What is the immediate opportunity?

A) Switch to hook sweep — they've stepped a knee up  
B) Kimura on the posted left hand ✓  
C) Triangle — their left arm is extended  
D) Sit-up sweep — their weight is now backward  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #66 — "opponent puts their hand on the floor" is the kimura trigger. The failed scissor sweep created this exact scenario. The posted arm trying to stop the sweep becomes the kimura target. This chain is one of the most common in guard work.  
**Why A is wrong:** No evidence of a stepped-up knee — that's a different reaction.  
**Why C is wrong:** Extended arm on the floor is a kimura target, not a triangle setup.  
**Why D is wrong:** Posting a hand creates forward energy — not rearward.

### Q77
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** #72, #67 | **Difficulty:** 3

You are in closed guard. You sit up for the sit-up sweep. Your opponent pushes back into you (forward energy). You naturally reach around their neck. What do you do?

A) Use the sit-up momentum to attempt the sweep anyway  
B) Circle your arm around their neck and set up the guillotine ✓  
C) Go back to guard and try a different sweep  
D) Attempt an armbar — their arm is extended  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #67 — "when you attempt to sweep and feel their weight pushing back at you." The sit-up sweep failure with forward energy is the textbook guillotine entry from guard. Your body is already up, their neck is accessible, their energy creates the choke direction.  
**Why A is wrong:** Forward energy directly counters the sit-up sweep direction.  
**Why C is wrong:** Resetting loses the forward energy that creates the guillotine.  
**Why D is wrong:** No arm isolation is described — the neck is the opportunity here.

### Q78

You are in your opponent's closed guard. You manage to posture up, establish choke protection, and begin the kneeling guard pass. As you bring your knee to the centerline, they attempt to pull you back down. What do you do?

A) Stop the pass — their grip is too strong  
B) Drive your hips forward and continue the knee-to-centerline pressure ✓  
C) Switch to the standing guard pass  
D) Sit back to avoid the pull  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #68 — the pass is committed at this point. The opponent pulling you back is the same force you resist with posture (#58). Drive hips forward, maintain back straight, continue the pass sequence. Commitment to the pass direction is the answer.  
**Why A is wrong:** Stopping and resetting gives the opponent time to reestablish their attack.  
**Why C is wrong:** Switching passes mid-sequence loses the positional advantage you've built.  
**Why D is wrong:** Sitting back returns to their guard control.

### Q79
**Type:** Concept | **Source:** #63, #64, #65, #66, #67 | **Difficulty:** 2

What is the strategic value of knowing MULTIPLE submissions from closed guard rather than mastering just one?

A) You can confuse your opponent by switching randomly  
B) Each submission has a different trigger and a failed attempt creates the next opportunity ✓  
C) Multiple submissions tire the opponent faster  
D) Competition rules give points for each submission attempt  

**Why B is correct:** This is the core of BJJ chain theory. Each technique in guard has a specific trigger AND a specific counter that creates the next technique's setup. Triangle defended → armbar. Scissor blocked → kimura. Kimura arm straightens → armbar. Knowing the chains is more valuable than isolated technique mastery.  
**Why A is wrong:** Random switching without reading the opponent's reaction is not effective strategy.  
**Why C is wrong:** Fatigue is a result — not the strategic reason.  
**Why D is wrong:** Competition points for attempts is not how BJJ scoring works.

**True or False:** If you fail a sweep attempt from closed guard, you have wasted your energy and should return to a passive guard position.

**Answer: FALSE ✓**

**Explanation:** A failed sweep attempt creates positional information and usually creates a submission opportunity. Scissor sweep blocked → kimura (opponent posts). Hook sweep fails → opponent's weight shifts → new opening. Sit-up sweep fails → opponent pushes forward → guillotine. Guard is an active conversation — a failed attack is not a reset, it's a transition.

**Why C is correct:** Pedro Sauer curriculum lists posture (#58) as the first guard defense technique. Danaher's curriculum begins with guard retention. **Why A is wrong:** Scissor sweep requires posture to defend against while setting up.

### Q83
**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** All Cycle 1 | **Difficulty:** 3

You are in closed guard against an opponent who is much stronger physically. They are maintaining good posture despite your attempts. Which approach is most likely to succeed?

A) Apply more force to your sweeps  
B) Increase intensity of collar grips to break posture by force  
C) Create a reaction with one technique and use their response for the next ✓  
D) Wait for them to tire out  

**Why C is correct:** This is the core principle of BJJ vs strength. Pedro Sauer's curriculum, Danaher, Saulo Ribeiro — all emphasize using the opponent's reaction rather than matching force. Triangle attempt → they posture up → kimura entry. Scissor attempt → they post → kimura. Guillotine from guard → they tighten up → back to sweep. Force vs force favors the stronger person.  
**Why A is wrong:** More force against a stronger person rarely works — this is why BJJ exists.  
**Why B is wrong:** More grip strength meets their resistance — doesn't create technique.  
**Why D is wrong:** Pure passivity is not a BJJ strategy — and stronger opponents are often in better condition too.

B) Prevent the second grip/position before attacks are established ✓  
C) Let attacks develop and escape from bad positions  
D) Match the opponent's aggression with equal aggression  

**Why B is correct:** Pedro Sauer #59 — "prevent hip movement, block 2nd grip." The entire guard defense section is about prevention, not reaction. Block the second collar grip before the choke is set. Connect elbow to knees before the armbar develops. Wrap arm before guillotine is applied. Proactive prevention is always easier than reactive defense.  
**Why A is wrong:** Reactive defense puts you a step behind — you're always fighting the developed attack.  
**Why C is wrong:** Escaping from fully established submissions is the hardest path.  
**Why D is wrong:** Aggression vs aggression is not the principle — prevention is.

As a top player trying to pass guard, what is the shared principle between the kneeling pass and the standing pass?

B) Both require establishing posture first, then using your weight to create pass direction ✓  
C) Both work better against opponents with strong closed guard  
D) Both require grabbing the collar to control opponent during the pass  

**Why B is correct:** Both passes begin with posture (#58) and use the principle of weight commitment to the pass direction. Kneeling: posture → knee to centerline → shoulder pass with weight forward. Standing: posture → double lapel → stand → drag under → weight commitment. The principle is the same — posture enables the weight transfer that creates the pass.  
**Why A is wrong:** Breaking posture is what the guard player does to the top player — not the other way around.  
**Why C is wrong:** Both passes work regardless of guard strength — they address guard mechanics, not strength.  
**Why D is wrong:** Standing pass uses double lapel grip — kneeling uses inside arm and lapel on shoulder leg — different grips.

Your opponent is in your closed guard. They have excellent posture. You try the scissor sweep but they base well. You try the sit-up sweep but they push forward. Now you have them in a guillotine from guard. They are defending by posturing their head up. What is the correct finish?

**Why A is correct:** Pedro Sauer #67 / #42 — "close guard and apply choke." Pulling the head down keeps the choke angle. The body and closed guard provide the squeeze force. Head up defense is countered by pulling it down — this is the finish mechanic.  
**Why B is wrong:** Opening guard removes the hip control that is 50% of the guillotine pressure.  
**Why C is wrong:** Transition from a live guillotine to triangle loses the primary attack unnecessarily.  
**Why D is wrong:** The guillotine has momentum from the previous techniques — abandoning it resets everything.

Posture is the first line of defense that protects all subsequent defenses.

**Why A is wrong:** Scissor first regardless of opponent's position means low percentage attempts.

**Type:** Situational Choice | **Source:** All Cycle 1 | **Difficulty:** 3

Final synthesis: You are in closed guard. In under 30 seconds your opponent has: broken your posture, gotten one hand in the collar, and is reaching for the second. What is the correct emergency sequence?

A) Immediately try to open their guard and stand up  
B) First restore posture (drive hips), then block second grip with arm over their arm ✓  
C) Grab their collar grip with both hands and force it out
