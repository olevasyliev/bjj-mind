### UNIT 1: Intro to Survival

---

**Q-1-01**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `escapes` `base` | Difficulty: 1

Position: You're on your back. Opponent is pushing into you from the side.
> **You need to create distance. What movement do you use?**
- A) Upa (bridge) ✅ — NO wait. Shrimp is for creating distance. Upa is for reversing.

Let me redo this properly:

> **You're on your back. Opponent is pushing into you. To move your hips AWAY and create space, you use:**
- A) Shrimp ✅
- B) Upa (bridge)

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** The shrimp moves your hips away from pressure — it's your primary tool for creating space from the bottom. Upa reverses position by bridging under the opponent.

---

**Q-1-02**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `base` `escapes` | Difficulty: 1

Position: Opponent is mounted on you. You want to reverse them.
> **You bridge (Upa). Before you bridge, what do you do with your head?**
- A) Tuck chin to chest
- B) Slide it back off center and look behind ✅

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Sliding the head back and off-center takes your spine out of alignment with theirs — this is what makes the bridge effective. Tucking your chin keeps you centered and the bridge goes nowhere.

---

**Q-1-03**
Format: `truefalse` | Tags: `base` | Difficulty: 1

> **True or False: During the Teeter Totter movement, it's okay to let your legs go past 90 degrees from your body.**

**Correct:** FALSE
**Explanation:** Legs past 90° means you've lost your base. The Teeter Totter teaches hip rotation while keeping legs in a safe, controlled range.

---

**Q-1-04**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `base` `self-defense` | Difficulty: 1

Position: You're on the ground. Opponent is standing and backing away. You need to get up safely.
> **As you Stand Up in Base, what do you do with your arm?**
- A) Keep both arms at sides for balance
- B) Block the punch line with one arm ✅

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Standing up in base without covering the punch line leaves your head exposed. The blocking arm protects you while you get to your feet.

---

**Q-1-05**
Format: `mcq4` | Tags: `escapes` | Difficulty: 1

> **Shrimp has 3 types. Which of these is NOT one of them?**
- A) Both feet push
- B) Inside foot
- C) Outside foot
- D) Crossbody shrimp ✅

**Correct:** D
**Explanation:** The 3 shrimp types are: both feet, inside foot, and outside foot. There is no "crossbody" variation in the fundamentals.

### UNIT 2: Frames

---

**Q-2-01**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `frames` `escapes` | Difficulty: 1

Position: Opponent is in side control on top of you. You want to regain guard.
> **The most important first move is:**
- A) Turn into them and push with one arm
- B) Get BOTH arms across your body to create the frame ✅

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** One arm doesn't create enough space. Both arms across forces a wedge between you, making it possible to insert the knee and recover guard.

---

**Q-2-02**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `frames` `escapes` | Difficulty: 2

Position: You're in side control on the bottom. You regain guard attempt — you've got both arms across. What next?
> **After framing with both arms across, you:**
- A) Shoot the hip away and insert the knee ✅
- B) Roll toward the opponent to escape

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** The knee insert is the guard recovery. Rolling toward them just gives them the space to take mount.

---

**Q-2-03**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `frames` | Difficulty: 1

Position: Opponent has a Front Bear Hug — arms are free (under your arms).
> **Your first move is:**
- A) Frame their face and drop back ✅
- B) Bridge and roll

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** When arms are free with a front bear hug, the frame on the face creates distance and disrupts their base. The bridge works when arms are pinned.

---

**Q-2-04**
Format: `truefalse` | Tags: `frames` `escapes` | Difficulty: 2

> **True or False: In the Elbow Escape (shrimp to guard), you should try to recover both sides of the guard at the same time.**

**Correct:** FALSE
**Explanation:** You recover one side at a time — shrimp one way, get one knee in, then adjust. Trying both sides at once splits your focus and strength.

---

**Q-2-05**

- B) Twisting out to the side
- C) Sitting up into them
- D) Pulling on their sleeves

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** Upa is the foundational mount escape. It uses your entire body (bridge) against their upper body — leverage wins over strength.

**Q-2-06**

**Explanation:** The knee is a frame — a structural barrier that keeps the opponent from re-closing the space. This is the core concept of framing.

**Q-2-08**

**Q-3-01**

> **You escape by pulling:**
- A) Straight back toward yourself
- B) Between their thumb and fingers ✅

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The weakest point of any grip is where the thumb meets the fingers. Pulling there breaks the grip with minimum force.

> **Your escape: grab your OWN wrist with the free hand, then:**
- A) Pull both arms back toward you ✅
- B) Rotate your wrists outward

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** Grabbing your own wrist connects both arms into one unit. Pulling back doubles your leverage against their grip.

> **True or False: In back mount control, keeping your hooks (feet) together is the safest position.**

**Correct:** FALSE
**Explanation:** Hooks together means your base is narrow and easy to escape. Feet apart with pressure against the femurs is the correct back control position.

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The seatbelt (backpack) grip with one over, one under gives you the best control and is the standard back mount control position.

**Q-3-06**

**Q-3-07**

**Q-3-08**

**Correct:** B

- C) At 90 degrees ✅
- D) At 45 degrees

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** The Kimura requires the arm at 90° to apply rotational pressure on the shoulder. Not 45°, not any angle — 90° is the mechanical requirement for the lock.

**Explanation:** You grab the FAR lapel — reaching across their body. This creates the leverage to drive them onto their side so you can establish the armlock. Near lapel doesn't give you the angle.

### UNIT 10: Guard Attacks

---

**Q-10-01**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `submissions` `grips` | Difficulty: 1

Position: You're in closed guard. Opponent is postured up. You go for the Cross Choke (technique 63).
> **Before attacking, you need to:**
- A) Break their posture first — pull their head down ✅
- B) Grip the collar while they're postured and squeeze

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** Breaking posture is mandatory before any guard attack. Against a postured opponent, their base is too strong. Break posture first, then attack.

---

**Q-10-02**
Format: `mcq4` | Tags: `submissions` `control` `timing` | Difficulty: 2

Position: You're in closed guard. You set up the Triangle Choke (technique 65).
> **Before locking the triangle, you must:**
- A) Pull their head down immediately
- B) Push one arm across their body and angle out to the side ✅
- C) Open your guard and swing legs up
- D) Underhook one arm

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The angle-out (turning your hips to the side) is what makes the triangle work. Without it, the legs don't lock correctly and the choke is incomplete. Push one arm across AND angle out.

---

**Q-10-03**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `submissions` `grips` | Difficulty: 2

Position: You're in closed guard. You try the Kimura (technique 66).
> **After getting the figure four grip, you:**
- A) Squeeze immediately
- B) Elbow to armpit, scoot hips away to isolate the arm, cross feet over their back ✅

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Technique 66 — elbow to armpit disconnects their arm from their body. Scooting away isolates it. Cross feet over the back keeps them from posturing. Then apply.

---

**Q-10-04**
Format: `truefalse` | Tags: `submissions` `timing` | Difficulty: 2

> **True or False: The Armbar from guard (technique 64) requires raising your hips to apply the lock.**

**Correct:** TRUE
**Explanation:** Hips up = lever. The armbar works by hyperextending the elbow — which requires the hips to bridge up against the arm. No hips = no lock.

---

**Q-10-05**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `submissions` `grips` | Difficulty: 2

Position: You're in closed guard attacking with the Guillotine Choke (technique 67).
> **You maintain distance from the opponent by:**
- A) Keeping your feet crossed and pulling them close
- B) Keeping knee to their mid-section ✅

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Knee to mid-section manages distance — it keeps them from smashing in and killing the choke angle. Feet crossed just holds them but doesn't manage the space.

---

**Q-10-06**
Format: `fill` | Tags: `submissions` `timing` | Difficulty: 3

> **In the guard armbar, keeping your toes pointed _____ tightens the leg clamp and prevents the arm from escaping.**
- Word bank: up / sideways / down / outward

**Correct:** up
**Explanation:** Toes pointed up rotates the leg inward, tightening the squeeze on the arm. This is the key cue for both guard and mount armbars.

---

**Q-10-07**
Format: `spotmistake` | Tags: `submissions` | Difficulty: 3

> **Alex has guard and goes for the Kimura. He gets the figure four but doesn't scoot his hips away. The opponent powers out. What was the problem?**
- A) He needed a different grip
- B) He didn't isolate the arm — without scooting away, the arm stays connected to the body ✅
- C) He should have gone for the armbar instead

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Technique 66 requires scooting away to isolate the arm. If you stay close, the opponent's body strength supports the arm and they can bench-press out of the Kimura.

### UNIT 12: Guard Passing & Posture

**Q-12-01**
Format: `mcq2` | Tags: `control` `frames` | Difficulty: 1

Position: You're in someone's closed guard on top.
> **The most important single thing you must establish first:**
- A) Break their guard open
- B) Posture — sit tall, hips driving toward the ground ✅

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Posture is everything in the guard. Without it, every attack hits harder, every sweep is easier. Sit tall, one arm post, hips down. Then you can think about passing.

**Q-12-02**

- B) Elbows outside your body ✅
- C) Knee not at centerline
- D) Passing to the wrong side

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Elbows inside = protected. Elbows outside = your arms are exposed for sweeps and armlocks. Keeping elbows inside while passing is a foundational guard passing concept.

**Q-12-03**

> **After grabbing both lapels and standing, you:**
- A) Jump over their legs
- B) Drag them under you, pinch legs, use elbow to open guard ✅

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Technique 69 — drag under + pinch legs is the key action sequence. "Jumping over" is an instinct that doesn't work — you need to control their legs by pinching and managing them.

**Q-12-04**

> **True or False: In the kneeling guard pass, you put the opponent's leg on your shoulder and then drop your weight to complete the pass.**

**Correct:** TRUE
**Explanation:** Technique 68 — leg on shoulder, drop weight forward, establish cross body. The weight drop is what pins the leg and completes the transition.

**Q-12-05**

**Explanation:** Arm bar protection from guard (technique 62) and general base work both point to connecting elbows to knees and posting arms. Basing out with arms interrupts the sweep. Standing is often the right call but takes setup.

## COACH MOMENTS (Unit-level principles)

> These are Marco / Old Chen interrupts — deeper concepts to teach between questions.

---

**CM-01** | Unit 2 | Tag: `frames`
> **"A frame is not a push — it's a structure."**
> When you push, you give up position the moment they push back. When you frame — elbow locked, bones stacked — you create a wall. Their strength goes into the structure, not into moving you. Frame first, escape second.

---

**CM-02** | Unit 1 | Tag: `escapes` `base`
> **"The shrimp is your most important move on the ground."**
> If you only learned one thing in BJJ, it would be the shrimp. Every escape, every guard recovery, every position change — all of them have shrimp inside them. When you don't know what to do on the bottom: shrimp.

---

**CM-03** | Unit 5 | Tag: `base`
> **"Bridge with your whole body, not your neck."**
> Beginners bridge with their neck and shoulders. The Upa is a FULL BODY movement — hips drive to the ceiling, your body rotates. The hips have the power. The neck is just along for the ride.

---

**CM-04** | Unit 7 | Tag: `submissions`
> **"The paintbrush is a technique inside a technique."**
> Before any arm lock from top, you must own the arm. The paintbrush slides it flat — like painting the floor with their forearm. Without this, you're fighting their strength. With it, their arm is already lost.

---

**CM-05** | Unit 9 | Tag: `submissions` `grips`
> **"Row with your back — not your arms."**
> The Rear Naked Choke fails for most beginners because they squeeze with their arms. The finishing motion is a ROW — elbows toward each other, back muscles engaging. Biceps are weak. Your back is strong.

---

**CM-06** | Unit 10 | Tag: `submissions`
> **"Break posture first. Always."**
> Every guard attack has the same prerequisite: their posture is broken. Collar choke, triangle, armbar — none of them work against a man sitting tall. Break posture first. Then your attacks become effortless.

---

**CM-07** | Unit 3 | Tag: `grips`
> **"Every grip has a weak side — and it's always the thumb."**
> When someone grabs you, their fingers form a ring. The weakest point is where the thumb closes. Pull toward the thumb gap, and any grip breaks. This works on wrists, collars, lapels — everything.

---

**CM-08** | Unit 12 | Tag: `control`
> **"Posture in guard is a position, not just a feeling."**
> Posture means: spine straight, hips low and pressing, one arm posted, chin up. Not just "trying to sit up." Each of those four things matters. Lose one and the attacks start working.
